# Creative Writing

Creative writing rewards specificity, restraint, and revision. The first draft
exists to find the story; every draft after exists to remove what isn't it.
This skill complements `docs/the-longform-guide.md` (long-form discipline)
and `docs/the-shortform-guide.md` (short-form punch).

## When to Activate

- Drafting fiction (short story, novel chapter, flash)
- Personal essays, memoir, narrative non-fiction
- Revising scenes for voice / pacing / POV
- Choosing between outlining vs discovery drafting
- Korean <-> English literary writing (translation, original in either)

## Voice First

Voice = diction + rhythm + the things the narrator notices.

- **Diction**: word range. A narrator who says "luminous" doesn't say "fancy"
  in the next paragraph unless the contrast is the point.
- **Rhythm**: sentence length variance. Short. Then a long one that builds
  through subordinate clauses to a turn at the end. Then short again.
- **Attention**: what the narrator points the camera at. The detail you
  choose is the character.

If the voice doesn't feel found, write 500 words in first person about a meal,
a commute, a fight you remember. Voice surfaces in low-stakes scenes faster
than in plotted ones.

## Scene-Level Checklist

A scene that's working has all five:

1. **POV is consistent** — pick a head, stay in it. POV slips happen at
   moments of dramatic tension; that's where the writer wants to cheat.
2. **The viewpoint character has a desire in the scene** (not just the story).
   "I want to leave without crying" is a scene desire.
3. **Conflict** — internal, interpersonal, or environmental. Without it, the
   scene is exposition wearing a costume.
4. **Sensory specificity** — at least one detail that couldn't be in any
   other scene in any other book. Generic = forgettable.
5. **Change** — the character, relationship, or reader's understanding is
   different at the end than the start. If nothing changed, cut the scene
   or merge it.

## Show vs Tell — The Useful Version

The "show, don't tell" rule is too crude. Better:

- **Tell facts**, **show experience**.
  "He'd been a Marine." (tell) → "He still made his bed in 30 seconds." (show)
- **Tell time / setup**, **show emotional beats**.
  "Three years passed." is fine. "She was angry." is lazy.
- Telling is faster. Use it for compression. Show only what carries weight.

## Revision Passes (in order)

Doing these out of order wastes work. Don't line-edit a scene you're going
to cut.

1. **Structure pass**: Does the story have a shape? Inciting incident, escalation,
   climax, resolution? Cut/move scenes. Add the scene that's missing.
2. **Scene pass**: For each scene, does it pass the checklist above?
   Strengthen the desire / conflict / change.
3. **Voice / dialogue pass**: Does each character sound distinguishable?
   Read dialogue aloud — if you can't tell who's talking with the tags
   covered, fix it.
4. **Line pass**: Sentence rhythm, word choice, redundancy. Cut adverbs that
   modify already-precise verbs. Replace "very X" with the correct word.
5. **Polish pass**: Typos, continuity (eye color, names, dates), formatting.
   Fresh eyes catch what tired eyes wrote.

Save full drafts between passes. Revision regret is real.

## Outline vs Discovery Drafting

- **Outline first** when:
  the structure is load-bearing (mystery, thriller, complex timeline);
  you've abandoned three drafts of this story already;
  you have a deadline.
- **Discover first** when:
  you don't yet know what the story is about;
  the voice is more important than the plot;
  early drafts in this genre have always died from over-engineering.

Hybrid: outline 3–5 anchor scenes (opening, midpoint, climax). Discover the
connections.

## Dialogue

- **Beats over tags.** "He said," is a tax on attention. Replace with a
  physical beat that carries subtext.
  > "It's fine." She wiped a knife clean.
- **No two characters explain the plot to each other** unless one of them
  is lying.
- **Subtext = what they want vs what they say.** Two people arguing about
  the dishwasher are usually arguing about something else.
- Dialogue tags: `said` is invisible and good. `exclaimed`, `retorted`,
  `intoned` are speed bumps.

## Korean <-> English Literary Writing

- **Sentence length**: Korean tolerates much longer sentences than English
  prose; subordinate clauses chain naturally. English literary prose
  prefers shorter, punchier units. Translating literally produces baggy
  English or choppy Korean — restructure at the sentence level.
- **Honorifics & speech levels (한국어)** carry character / status / intimacy
  information. In English, lean on word choice and contraction patterns
  ("won't" vs "will not") to do the same work.
- **Onomatopoeia (의성어/의태어)** is a load-bearing literary device in
  Korean (찰랑찰랑, 두근두근). English doesn't translate these directly;
  rewrite the *image*, not the sound.
- **POV markers**: Korean often omits subject; English requires it. When
  translating Korean fiction, decide where the absence is the point and
  preserve it via syntax (sentence fragments, passive voice) rather than
  inventing a subject.
- **Cultural specificity is the asset.** Don't smooth out 동네 슈퍼 to
  "convenience store"; let the specific stay specific.

## Banned Patterns

Delete on sight:

- **"He felt a wave of [emotion] wash over him."** Cliché made of three
  clichés.
- **Mirror exposition** — character looks at reflection to deliver
  description. There's almost always a better moment.
- **Dream openings** — "She woke up." Resented since 1985.
- **Weather-only openings** — set place + time + a character noticing
  something *about* the weather.
- **Adverb stacking** — "she said angrily and dismissively." Pick one or
  rewrite the line.
- **"Suddenly,"** — usually a sign the action wasn't built up properly.
- **AI-flavored prose**: smooth abstractions, no specifics, every metaphor
  half-fresh. The cure is concrete nouns and verbs.

## Reading As Practice

Read in the genre you're writing, with a notebook. When a scene works,
ask:
- What did the writer *not* say?
- Where did time speed up or slow down?
- What was the camera looking at?

Imitate technique, not voice. Voice is yours; technique is how you spend it.
